Part II - Do Your Friends Give You Better Student Loan Deals?¶

by Isadore Johnson¶

Investigation Overview¶

Overall, my project looked at whether having higher amounts of friends as investors in your student loan would get you a better deal, either in term length, or expected yield.

Insights:

There are no strong correlations between Term Length and Estimated Yield

There is no strong correlation between Term Length or FriendInvestorCount.

There is a weak negative correlation between Friend Investor Count and Estimated Yield but could be a result of narrowing ranges.

student loans are overwhelmingly not financed by friends

Dataset Overview and Executive Summary¶

The dataset contains 81 variables, and looks at student loan data. There are 113,937 loans to use as data points. I look at the variables Term, EstimatedEffectiveYield, and InvestmentFromFriendsCount.

Estimated Effective Yield by Investment From Friends Count¶

As you increase the Investment From Friends Count, you start to notice the range narrow slightly, and appear to move slightly down, implying a very noisy negative correlation. You can also see several outliers in terms of several negative yield situations with zero friends.

Heatmap of Investment From Friends Count vs Term¶

I thought this heatmap was insightful because it shows just how concentrated the value of zero is for Investment From Friends Count. Similarly, Term of 36 was most concentrated, and looked like 12 was the least frequent term.
